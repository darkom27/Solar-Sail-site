Engineering Analysis

A critical engineering and physics analysis of the static solar thruster (Shkadov Thruster), identifying the fundamental obstacles that separate theoretical elegance from practical reality.

1. Theoretical Foundation

The starting point of the analysis was the application of Newton's Third Law at the photon level. Although photons have no rest mass, they carry momentum defined by:

p = E / c

Where p is momentum, E is photon energy, and c is the speed of light. Upon impact with an ideal mirror, a photon transfers momentum 2p (stopping and being re-emitted in the opposite direction).

The resulting radiation pressure on a perfectly reflecting surface is:

P_rad = 2I / c

Where I is the irradiance (W/m²). At the solar surface, this is approximately 63.3 MW/m², yielding a radiation pressure of ≈ 0.42 N/m² for a perfect reflector.

Key insight: At the Sun's surface gravity of 274 m/s², for radiation pressure to overcome gravity, the mirror's areal mass density must remain below P_rad / g_sun ≈ 1.53 mg/m². This is thinner than a soap bubble.

For the purposes of simulation and analysis, a full-scale model was defined with the following parameters, mirroring a realistic proposal for a minimal Shkadov mirror:

The critical problem identified at these dimensions is the mass balance paradox: any material thick enough to be structurally stable will be too heavy to be levitated by radiation pressure alone.

3. Photon–Surface and Photon–Photon Interactions

style="margin-top:0;">A. Photon on Surface (Vibrational Effect)

Every photon impact on a material atom (e.g. graphene) is not simply "a ball bouncing off a wall." It is an energy transfer event that causes:

  • Electron excitation — electrons temporarily jump to higher energy states.
  • Phonon generation — crystal lattice vibrations, manifesting as heat.
  • Localised stress — at extreme flux, atomic bonds are strained beyond elastic limits.

In vacuum, these vibrational energies accumulate because there is no convective cooling. The only thermal release mechanism is blackbody radiation — which is insufficient at the power densities involved.

style="margin-top:0;">B. Photon–Photon Interference (30° Sector)

Within the 30° coverage zone, incoming solar beams and outgoing reflected beams create an extremely high electromagnetic energy density. This activates several QED processes:

  • Delbrück scattering — photon-photon interaction mediated by virtual electron-positron pairs.
  • Vacuum birefringence — the polarisation state of light is modified by the intense EM field.
  • Standing wave formation — interference nodes convert ordered kinetic energy into chaotic thermal noise.

These effects degrade the net thrust vector, reducing effective momentum transfer below the idealised theoretical value.

Quantification note: Delbrück scattering cross-sections are extremely small at optical frequencies, but at solar surface irradiances (~63 MW/m²), the cumulative effect over the reflective surface area becomes non-negligible. The interference standing waves are the more dominant practical problem, redistributing energy spatially.

4. Thermodynamic Collapse

The most significant engineering objection to the theoretical model is its dismissal of the Second Law of Thermodynamics in vacuum. Without convection (air), the mirror becomes an insulated thermos bottle. Consider the following:

  1. Heat input: At 99.9% reflectivity, the absorbed power density is 0.1% × 63.3 MW/m² = 63,300 W/m².
  2. Equilibrium calculation: Using Stefan-Boltzmann: P = εσT⁴, setting ε = 0.9 (emissivity), the equilibrium temperature is T = (63,300 / (0.9 × 5.67×10⁻⁸))^0.25 ≈ 2,090 K (≈ 1,820°C).
  3. Material failure: Graphene theoretically survives to ~3,000 K in vacuum, but real polycrystalline graphene films or composite mirrors fail structurally well before that — typically between 900–1,500 K due to grain boundary diffusion, sublimation of impurities, and rippling instabilities.
  4. Time to failure: For a mirror of areal mass ~1 mg/m² with heat capacity ~700 J/kg·K, absorbing 63.3 kW/m², the temperature would rise at approximately 90,000 K/second before radiative cooling activates. Operational window: milliseconds.
ΔT/Δt = P_absorbed / (m_area × c_p) = 63,300 W/m² / (0.001 kg/m² × 700 J/kg·K) ≈ 90,430 K/s

This is not a solvable engineering problem with current materials. Even at 2.8 AU from the Sun (where irradiance drops to ~174 W/m²), the equilibrium temperature for a 60% reflectivity mirror still exceeds the softening point of most known refractory materials.

6. Force Balance and Net Thrust

The radiation pressure on a perfect reflector positioned at solar distance R is given by:

F_thrust = (2 × I × A × η) / c

Where A is mirror area, η is reflectivity, and I is solar irradiance at distance R.

For our R=50m model at the solar surface (I = 63.3 MW/m²):

F_thrust = (2 × 63.3×10⁶ × π×50² × 0.999) / (3×10⁸) ≈ 66,300 N (66.3 kN)

This sounds substantial — but the solar gravitational force on the same mirror mass at the solar surface (g_sun = 274 m/s²) is:

For F_thrust = F_gravity: m/A = F_thrust / (g × A) = (2I×η) / (c × g_sun) = (2 × 63.3×10⁶ × 0.999) / (3×10⁸ × 274) ≈ 1.54 mg/m²
The conflict is absolute: No physical material that can serve as a functional optical mirror at these temperatures has an areal density below 1.54 mg/m². Even graphene layers would require extreme reinforcement structures that push mass far above this limit. The equation is not solvable with known chemistry.

style="margin-top:0; color:var(--gold);">Conclusions

The Shkadov Thruster is a concept that is theoretically consistent with the fundamental laws of physics, but reveals itself as impractical under detailed engineering analysis. The principal limitations are not mathematical in nature — they arise from:

  1. Vacuum thermodynamics: no convective cooling means even tiny absorption fractions lead to catastrophic heating.
  2. Material limits: the areal density requirement is incompatible with optical functionality and structural integrity in any known material.
  3. Nonlinear quantum effects: Delbrück scattering and interference-induced standing waves reduce practical thrust below theoretical values.

Final assessment: Theoretically functional. Engineeringly unusable — until materials science makes a breakthrough of several orders of magnitude.
